Do Pharmaceutical Companies Publish Biased Research?

In 2019 I had the opportunity to share some of my research at the American Academy of Neurologists in Philadelphia. Here I’ll provide my poster as well as a few reasons for my excitement about the topic of literature bias in Alzheimer’s Disease.

What This Research Contributes:

  • The largest review of Alzheimer’s literature focusing on bias
  • Expands on previous Funding bias research in Alzheimer’s Disease
  • Provides non-anecdotal exploration into Publishing Journal as a source of bias
  • Introduces Author Affiliations as a potential biasing factor

Background

The concept of literature bias is not new. However, most research on this topic studies internal factors such as biased groups, randomization, or unfair treatment comparisons. I wanted to investigate the impact that external factors could have on reported results. The final set of external factors were found in the metadata of studies and included Funding Source, Author Affiliations, and Publishing Journal.

Funding Bias

Of these three factors, Funding bias has been investigated the most with a few dozen studies over the past decade looking into it in a variety of disease areas. Results of these studies are mixed and still very sparse in Alzheimer’s Disease. In the past, I have been cuationed against trusting pharmaceutically sponsored research due to the amount of money invested in the success of drugs - this makes sense, anecdotally, but I had never seen any evidence to prove it.

Journal Bias

Journals can introduce bias in two major ways:

  1. Only publishing “flashy” articles that show how great treatments are. (Who wants to read another null or negative result?)
  2. Predatory journals publishing studies without a peer-review process or other quality control.

Given that I wanted to have large sample sizes for each subgroup, I was unable to study predatory journals here and focused on reputable journals that commonly publish Alzheimer’s research.

Author Affiliations

I was unable to find any previous research studying the potential effects of author affiliation on reported outcomes. This was a surprise as I suspsect that affiiliations and study sponsors impart pressure on the authors to succeed in their trials.
